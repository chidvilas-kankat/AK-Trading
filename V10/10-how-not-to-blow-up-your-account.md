### 2 · Problem / Agitation
> "Here's the uncomfortable truth almost no course will tell you: **you can be right about the market and still lose all your money.**
>
> Picture it. You take a trade you're sure about and put a huge chunk of your account on it, because why not — you're *confident*. It goes against you. Instead of cutting the loss, you hold (it'll come back), maybe even add more. One bad position, and weeks of progress are gone. It wasn't your analysis that ruined you — it was your *bet size* and your lack of a loss limit. This is how the overwhelming majority of accounts actually die: not from one wrong prediction, but from risking too much, too often, with no plan for being wrong. And being wrong is guaranteed — even the best traders are wrong constantly."

### 3 · Reframe / Opportunity
> "So let's change the goal. The aim of trading isn't to be right. It's to **survive long enough for your edge to play out** — and that's a skill you can actually learn and control, unlike the market itself.
>
> You can't control whether any single trade wins. You *can* control exactly how much you risk on it. Professionals obsess over this. They decide, before entering, the precise amount they're willing to lose. They size every position so that a string of losses — which *will* happen — can't take them out. They cap their losses for the day and walk away when they hit it. It's not glamorous and it doesn't sell courses, but **risk management is the closest thing to an edge that exists** — and it's the first thing we teach, not the last."

### 4 · Named Methodology — **The Risk‑First Framework**
**Objective:** make capital preservation a mechanical, repeatable discipline — decided *before* emotion enters.

Protect first, profit second — five rules in order:
1. **Decide the risk before the trade.** Define your maximum loss on a position *before* you enter — never after, never "I'll see how it goes".
2. **Size to the stop, not the hope.** Your position size is set by where you'll exit if you're wrong, not by how good you feel about being right.
3. **Think in R, not rupees.** Measure every trade in multiples of the amount you risked (your "R"), so wins and losses are comparable and ego stays out of it.
4. **Cap the bleed.** Set a daily/weekly loss limit and *stop* when you hit it. The ability to walk away is a skill, and it's what keeps a bad day from becoming a blown account.
5. **Survive the drawdown.** Understand the math of losing streaks and recovery, so you size small enough that no normal run of losses can ever ruin you.

> Microcopy: *"None of these tell you what to buy. They make sure that when you're wrong — and you will be — it never costs you the game."*

## Version‑specific UI/UX direction

- **Accent strategy:** the **calmest, most precise** layout in the pack. Heavy whitespace deliberately signals safety and control. Keep the navy authoritative; use the **danger‑red (`--danger`)** *intentionally and sparingly* — as the colour of the thing you're defending against (the blowing‑up curve, the "max loss" marker) — while calm green/navy represent the protected, surviving state. Colour does semantic work here.
- **Signature motif: the shield.** A clean shield mark recurs as the brand cue for "protected capital" — in the hero, beside the §4 rules, and on the calculator. Pair it with simple, exact line diagrams (equity curves, a stop‑loss diagram, an R‑multiple ruler).
- **Type:** precise and trustworthy — **IBM Plex Sans** or **Inter** headlines; **IBM Plex Mono** for any numbers, R‑multiples, percentages and calculator output (mono communicates "precise / measured"). This shares the quant flavour of v07 but stays calmer and more defensive than data‑driven.
- **Hero composition:** the dual equity‑curve diagram (one surviving, one blowing up) or the shield, with abundant negative space around the H1. Restraint *is* the message — a calm page about not panicking.
- **Imagery & icons:** diagrammatic over photographic — shields, curves, gauges, a position‑size dial. If using photos, keep them calm and considered, never adrenaline‑charged trading‑floor imagery.
- **The calculator is the centrepiece.** The interactive Position‑Size Calculator isn't a footnote — feature it prominently as the page's signature "try it now" moment (see build notes).
- **Micro‑interactions:** subtle and reassuring. The risk‑of‑ruin table can highlight rows as you adjust an input; the equity‑curve diagram can animate gently to show the surviving line outlasting the volatile one. Nothing flashing or frantic.
- **Mobile:** sticky "Protect Your Capital →"; the calculator must be fully usable one‑handed on mobile (large inputs, instant output); diagrams stack and stay legible.

## Version‑specific build notes
- **Build the Position‑Size Calculator as a real interactive widget** — the standout lead magnet and on‑theme proof of value. Inputs: account size, % willing to risk, entry, stop. Output: suggested position size and the rupee risk. **Frame it strictly as an educational tool**, with a visible note that it's for learning, uses illustrative inputs, and is not advice or a recommendation to trade.
- A simple **Risk‑of‑Ruin / drawdown‑recovery table** (static or lightly interactive) powerfully demonstrates "why small risk = survival" — a memorable, screenshot‑worthy teaching asset.
- Keep the build light and fast; the calm aesthetic depends on a clean, uncluttered, quick‑loading page. Don't drown the whitespace in widgets — the calculator and one table are enough.
- The mono‑numeral styling and diagrams can be plain CSS/SVG; prioritise legibility and mobile usability over visual flourish.

## Compliance flags (this version)
- **This is the most naturally compliant angle in the pack** — you're explicitly selling *risk reduction and survival*, not gains. Lean into that, but don't let it tip into an implied guarantee: managing risk lowers the chance of *ruin*, it does **not** promise profit. Keep "~9 in 10 lose / no guarantees" present.
- The **R‑multiple and reward talk must never become a forward promise** ("risk 1 to make 3 and you'll grow your account"). Teach the *concept* of measuring risk and reward; never project outcomes or returns.
- The **calculator and risk‑of‑ruin tools must carry an educational disclaimer** and use illustrative inputs — never phrase their output as a recommendation, a guaranteed safe size, or advice.
- No profit screenshots or P&L. Proof here is *method and control*, consistent with the rest of the pack.
- All examples ≥3 months old and clearly labelled as educational/historical.
